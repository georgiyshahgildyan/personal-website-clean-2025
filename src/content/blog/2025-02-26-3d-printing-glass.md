Additive manufacturing makes it possible to design porous scaffolds with controlled architecture, which is crucial for tissue engineering. When glass-based materials are used, scaffolds can also provide chemical cues through controlled dissolution and ion release. This combination—structure plus chemistry—is why glass scaffolds remain a major research direction.

The materials problem is achieving printability, mechanical stability, and biological function at the same time. Porosity must support transport and vascularization, but the structure must not collapse or dissolve too quickly. Processing must produce reproducible geometry while maintaining a surface state that supports the intended biological response.

From a broader glass research viewpoint, this field is exciting because it forces tight control of composition, microstructure, and processing. It also highlights why glass-based materials are not “old-school.” They are a flexible platform for next-generation biomedical engineering where function is designed at multiple scales.
