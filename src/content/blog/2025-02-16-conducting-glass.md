Glass is famous for being an electrical insulator, but its electrical behavior can vary widely depending on composition and temperature. Many oxide glasses suppress electronic conduction, which makes them excellent dielectrics. At the same time, the presence of mobile ions can enable ionic conductivity, especially as temperature increases or under strong electric fields.

This duality is why glass appears in topics like dielectric substrates, sensors, and even solid electrolytes. Electrical response is also frequency-dependent: different polarization mechanisms contribute at different timescales. As a result, permittivity and loss are not single numbers—they are behaviors that depend on conditions.

Impurities and defects can dramatically influence measurements. Trace transition metals can introduce absorption and hopping conduction, while moisture can alter surface conductivity and interfacial polarization. For glass researchers, electrical properties are one of the most direct ways to probe structure–property relationships.
