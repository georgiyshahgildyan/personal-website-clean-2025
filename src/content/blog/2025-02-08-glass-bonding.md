Glass is chemically stable, which is part of its appeal, but that same stability can make bonding difficult. In practice, strong bonding is rarely about “choosing the strongest adhesive.” It is about engineering the surface and the interface so the joint remains reliable after humidity exposure, thermal cycling, and mechanical load.

A glass surface can be hydrated, contaminated, or simply low-energy for wetting, and any of these conditions can limit adhesion. Surface preparation and activation can increase wettability and create reactive groups, which makes the interface more chemically connected rather than merely mechanically attached. This is why glass bonding is taught as an interfacial materials science problem.

Coupling chemistries are often used to build a molecular bridge between glass and polymers. Polymer coatings can also help by reducing stress concentration and improving compatibility. But bonding success still depends on thermal expansion mismatch and how stress is distributed through the joint during real service conditions.

This is a classic example of why glass research matters beyond optics: the behavior of the glass surface can define whether a device works for ten years or fails in ten weeks.
